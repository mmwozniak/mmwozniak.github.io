---

title: "Theoretical work on the self"

---

<p style="font-size: 80%;">Alongside empirical work, I develop theoretical frameworks for understanding what the self is, how its different components relate to one another, and how it develops over the lifespan. The self is studied across a wide range of disciplines — from philosophy and neuroscience to psychology, sociology, and political science — but these disciplines often operate with radically different conceptions of what "the self" means. My theoretical work attempts to bring clarity by showing how these different understandings relate to each other, especially in the context of the cognitive and neural mechanisms that underpin them.</p>

<h2>THE STRUCTURE OF THE SELF: "I" AND "ME"</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">In this theoretical paper I drew on the classical philosophical distinction between the "I" (the experiencing, acting subject) and the "Me" (the self as an object of reflection) and argued that these correspond to distinct cognitive processes and neural mechanisms. I proposed that consciousness plays a key role in integrating these two aspects of self-experience, and that dissociations between them — as seen in various psychiatric and neurological conditions — can illuminate the architecture of the self. This framework provides a foundation for understanding why manipulations that affect one aspect of self-processing (e.g., bodily self) do not necessarily affect others (e.g., abstract self-concept).</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Woźniak, M. (2018). <a href="https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.01656"  target="_blank">"'I' and 'Me': the self in the context of consciousness"</a>. <i>Frontiers in Psychology, 9</i>, 1656.</p>

<h2>HOW THE SELF DEVELOPS: A BAYESIAN BRAIN PERSPECTIVE</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">This paper proposes a developmental account of self-representation within the predictive processing / Bayesian brain framework. I argue that the self is not a given — it is a model that the brain builds and refines throughout development. In infancy and childhood, the brain gradually learns to distinguish self-generated from externally-caused sensory signals, to associate certain sensory patterns with "self" rather than "world", and to build increasingly abstract representations of one's own identity. I show how this developmental trajectory can account for the plasticity of adult self-representation — including the ability to rapidly self-associate with novel stimuli such as avatar faces or robotic bodies — and how disruptions at different developmental stages might lead to different forms of self-related psychopathology.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Woźniak, M. (2024). <a href="https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2024.1441931"  target="_blank">"How to grow a self: development of self-representation in the Bayesian brain"</a>. <i>Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 18</i>.</p>

<h2>SELF-CONCEPT PLASTICITY: AN EDITORIAL OVERVIEW</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">Together with Marek Van Buuren and Pawel Tacikowski, I edited a special issue reviewing behavioral and neural evidence on self-concept plasticity — the capacity of the self to change in response to experience. The editorial provides an overview of recent findings on this topic and situates them within broader theoretical debates about the stability and flexibility of self-representations across the lifespan.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Woźniak, M., Van Buuren, M. & Tacikowski, P. (2025). <a href="https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2025.1584910"  target="_blank">"Self-concept plasticity: Behavioral and neural evidence"</a>. <i>Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 19</i>, 1584910.</p>

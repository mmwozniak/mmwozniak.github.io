---

title: "We-representations and joint action"

---

<p style="font-size: 80%;">When two people act together towards a shared goal, they need to represent not only their own actions, but also those of their partner — and crucially, how the two sets of actions relate to one another. Research on joint action has proposed that the brain constructs so-called <i>we-representations</i>: representations of the features of a joint action that cannot be reduced to individual contributions alone. In a series of studies using behavioral methods, EEG, and robot teleoperation, we investigated how we-representations are formed, how they influence cognition, and what happens when two people jointly control a single body.</p>

<h2>STUDY 1: NEURAL EVIDENCE FOR WE-REPRESENTATIONS DURING JOINT ACTION PLANNING</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">In this EEG study, pairs of participants planned and executed joint actions together. We found that already before acting, neural signals reflected not just self- and partner-action representations, but information about the <i>joint</i> structure of the upcoming action — i.e., a we-representation. This early neural signature suggests that the brain proactively integrates information about both agents' contributions when planning a shared action.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Kourtis, D., Woźniak, M., Sebanz, N. & Knoblich, G. (2019). <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2019.05.029"  target="_blank">"Evidence for We-representations during joint action planning"</a>. <i>Neuropsychologia, 131</i>, 73–83.</p>

<h2>STUDY 2: ATTENTION ALLOCATION DURING JOINT ACTION PLANNING</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">An earlier study examined how attention is distributed between self- and other-relevant information during joint action planning. We found that people do not simply focus on their own upcoming actions, but also allocate attention to their partner's contributions in a way that is sensitive to the task structure. This flexible attention allocation is one of the mechanisms through which we-representations are built and maintained.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Kourtis, D., Knoblich, G., Woźniak, M. & Sebanz, N. (2014). <a href="https://doi.org/10.1162/jocn_a_00634"  target="_blank">"Attention Allocation and Task Representation during Joint Action Planning"</a>. <i>Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience, 26</i>(10), 2275–2286.</p>

<h2>STUDY 3: WE-INFORMATION FACILITATES PERFORMANCE IN JOINT ROBOT TELEOPERATION</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">We developed a setup in which two people simultaneously teleoperate a single humanoid robot, each controlling one arm. Before each movement, participants could receive "we-information" — a cue indicating whether their upcoming movements would be the same or different from their co-operator's. We found that we-information sped up performance especially for simpler movement configurations, and that joint control led to a pronounced blurring of the boundaries between self, the robot, and the co-operator (increased self-other overlap). These findings show that we-representations can form even in radically non-biological joint action contexts.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Woźniak, M., Ari, I., De Tommaso, D. & Wykowska, A. (2025). "We-information can facilitate performance in joint teleoperation over a humanoid robot". <i>34th IEEE International Conference on Robot and Human Interactive Communication (RO-MAN)</i>.</p>

<h2>STUDY 4: NEURAL CORRELATES OF WE-INFORMATION PROCESSING DURING SHARED ROBOT CONTROL</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">In a follow-up EEG study on the same joint teleoperation paradigm, we investigate the neural mechanisms through which we-information influences performance. We record EEG while two participants jointly control a humanoid robot and examine how the presence of we-information modulates early visual ERP components (P1, N1, P2p). Preliminary results suggest that joint action context affects low-level perceptual processing of task-relevant cues, and that the brain applies similar computational principles whether cooperating with a human partner or jointly controlling a robotic body. This work is currently in progress — a full report will be added when the paper becomes available.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;"><i>Woźniak, M., et al. "Two Minds, One Body: Neural correlates of processing of we-information during shared control over a humanoid robot" — work in progress.</i></p>

---

title: "We-representations and joint action"
excerpt: "When two people act together, they don't just represent their own actions — the brain builds a shared 'we-representation' of the joint action as a whole."

---

<p style="font-size: 80%;">When two people act together toward a shared goal, something remarkable happens in the brain: it doesn't just track "what I'm doing" and "what you're doing" separately. Research suggests it builds a combined representation of the joint action — a kind of "we-representation" that captures features of the collaboration as a whole. In a series of studies, we investigated where these representations come from, how they influence performance, and what happens when two people jointly control a single robotic body.</p>

<h2>STUDY 1 & 2: EVIDENCE FROM BRAIN SIGNALS DURING JOINT ACTION PLANNING</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">In two EEG studies, pairs of participants planned and performed joint actions together. We found that even before the action began, neural signals already reflected the structure of the <i>joint</i> action — not just each person's individual contribution. Participants also distributed their attention to their partner's upcoming movements in a way that tracked the demands of the shared task.</p>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">Kourtis, D., Knoblich, G., Woźniak, M. & Sebanz, N. (2014). <a href="https://doi.org/10.1162/jocn_a_00634" target="_blank">"Attention Allocation and Task Representation during Joint Action Planning"</a>. <i>Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience, 26</i>(10), 2275–2286.</p>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">Kourtis, D., Woźniak, M., Sebanz, N. & Knoblich, G. (2019). <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2019.05.029" target="_blank">"Evidence for We-representations during joint action planning"</a>. <i>Neuropsychologia, 131</i>, 73–83.</p>

<h2>STUDY 3: WHAT HAPPENS WHEN TWO PEOPLE CONTROL ONE ROBOT?</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">We built a setup where two participants simultaneously teleoperate a single humanoid robot — each controlling one of the robot's arms. Before each movement, they could receive "we-information": a cue telling them whether their upcoming arm movements would be the same or different from their co-operator's.</p>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">We-information sped up task performance — especially for simpler movement configurations. And after just a brief session of jointly controlling a single robot body, participants reported a striking blurring of boundaries: they felt less able to distinguish between themselves, the robot, and their co-operator.</p>

<h2>STUDY 4: HOW DOES THE BRAIN PROCESS WE-INFORMATION?</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">In an ongoing EEG study using the same joint teleoperation setup, we are recording brain activity to understand the neural basis of we-information processing. Preliminary results suggest that the mere appearance of the we-information cue modulates early visual brain responses — meaning the brain starts preparing for joint action very rapidly, before conscious deliberation. A full report will be added when the paper is complete.</p>

<p style="font-size: 80%;"><i>Woźniak, M., et al. "Two Minds, One Body: Neural correlates of processing of we-information during shared control over a humanoid robot" — work in progress.</i></p>

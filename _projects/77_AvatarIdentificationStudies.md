---

title: "What happens when I identify with an avatar face?"

---
<div style="font-size: 80%;">

<p>When we play computer games we often take control over the body of another person — Lara Croft, Geralt of Rivia, or one of a dozen characters from any fighting game. In such cases, we "inhabit" that other person's body, at least for as long as we continue playing. But what happens in our mind and brain in such situations? In a series of studies we tried to answer this question.</p>

<h2>STUDY 1: WE ARE FASTER TO DETECT OUR AVATAR FACE</h2>

<p>When participants were assigned an unfamiliar face as their "avatar" and briefly identified with it, they became faster at detecting that face compared to faces assigned to others or to a stranger. This suggests that even very short-term identification with an avatar face is sufficient to trigger the self-prioritization effect — the same cognitive advantage that our own real face normally enjoys.</p>
<p>Article: M. Woźniak & G. Knoblich (2019) <a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1747021819832981"  target="_blank">"Self-prioritization of fully unfamiliar stimuli"</a>. <i>Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology, 72</i>(8), 2110–2120.</p>

<h2>STUDY 2: OUR BRAIN PERCEIVES OUR AVATAR FACE SIMILARLY TO OUR REAL FACE...</h2>

<p>Using EEG, we found that brain responses to an avatar face closely resembled responses to participants' own real faces, and differed from responses to strangers' faces. This indicates that self-association with an avatar face changes not only reaction times, but also how the brain processes the visual appearance of that face at an early, perceptual level.</p>
<p>Article: M. Woźniak, D. Kourtis & G. Knoblich (2018) <a href="https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0190679"  target="_blank">"Prioritization of arbitrary faces associated to self: An EEG study"</a>. <i>PLoS ONE, 13</i>(1), e0190679.</p>

<h2>STUDY 3: ...BUT THESE SIMILARITIES ARE LIMITED</h2>

<img src="/assets/images/WH2020_Twitter_summary.png">

<p>While avatar self-association boosts detection of avatar faces, not all features of real-face processing are replicated. Top-down (conceptual) self-association drives the avatar advantage, but it does not fully replicate the bottom-up perceptual effects that accompany processing of one's actual face — such as the deeply ingrained familiarity with one's own appearance built up over a lifetime.</p>
<p>Article: M. Woźniak & J. Hohwy (2020) <a href="https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0235627"  target="_blank">"Stranger to my face: top-down and bottom-up effects underlying prioritization of images of one's face"</a>. <i>PLoS ONE, 15</i>(7): e0235627.</p>

<h2>STUDY 4: WHEN WE IDENTIFY WITH AN AVATAR FACE WE "ESTRANGE" OUR REAL FACE</h2>

<img src="/assets/images/Self_dissolution_001.jpg" width="30%" style="float:middle">

<p>In this study participants identified with an avatar face, and we then tested how their perception of their <i>own real face</i> changed. We found that after identifying with an avatar, people's perceptual representation of their own face was altered — their real face felt less "theirs". This suggests that avatar identification does not simply add a new self-representation, but actively reorganizes the existing one, causing a partial estrangement from one's own appearance.</p>
<p>Article: M. Woźniak, K. Friebe, G. Knoblich & L. Maister <a href="https://osf.io/preprints/psyarxiv/92vkb"  target="_blank">"Identifying with an avatar face changes perceptual representation of one's real face"</a> <i>(preprint)</i></p>

<h2>STUDY 5: ...AND AVATAR SELF-IDENTIFICATION MIGHT BE ASSOCIATED WITH DEPERSONALIZATION</h2>

<p>Depersonalization is a condition in which people feel detached from their own thoughts, feelings, and body — as if they were an outside observer of themselves. Strikingly, the "estrangement" from one's real face found in Study 4 resembles reports of people experiencing depersonalization. To investigate this connection directly, we studied whether people who score higher on depersonalization show a different pattern of self-prioritization. We found that depersonalization specifically disrupted the self-prioritization of <i>bodily</i> (face-related) information, while the prioritization of abstract self-associated information remained intact. This dissociation suggests that depersonalization selectively targets the bodily dimension of the self-representation, while leaving the more abstract, conceptual self relatively unaffected.</p>
<p>Article: M. Woźniak, L. McEllin, J. Hohwy & A. Ciaunica (2023) <a href="https://doi.org/10.1037/xhp0001153"  target="_blank">"Depersonalization affects self-prioritization of bodily, but not abstract self-related information"</a>. <i>Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance, 49</i>(11), 1447–1459.</p>
<p>Article: A. Ciaunica, L. McEllin, J. Kiverstein, V. Gallese, J. Hohwy & M. Woźniak (2022) <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-07657-8"  target="_blank">"Zoomed out: digital media use and depersonalization experiences during the COVID-19 lockdown"</a>. <i>Scientific Reports, 12</i>(1), 3888.</p>

---

title: "Depersonalization and self-representation"

---

<p style="font-size: 80%;">Depersonalization is a dissociative condition in which people feel estranged from their own thoughts, feelings, sensations, and body — as if they were observing themselves from the outside. While depersonalization is commonly experienced transiently (e.g., from fatigue or stress), it can become a chronic disorder that severely impacts quality of life. In this project we used depersonalization as a window into the structure of the self, asking: which aspects of self-representation are disrupted when the sense of "being oneself" breaks down?</p>

<h2>DEPERSONALIZATION AND DIGITAL MEDIA DURING COVID-19</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">During the COVID-19 lockdowns, millions of people suddenly shifted to spending the majority of their social lives through screens. This dramatic change in embodied social experience provided a unique opportunity to study how digital-mediated interaction affects the sense of self. In a large survey study, we found that increased digital media use during lockdown was associated with higher levels of depersonalization and derealization experiences. People who spent more time communicating via video calls and other screen-based media felt more detached from their own bodies and from the reality around them. These findings suggest that the embodied nature of face-to-face interaction plays an important role in maintaining a stable sense of self.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Ciaunica, A., McEllin, L., Kiverstein, J., Gallese, V., Hohwy, J. & Woźniak, M. (2022). <a href="https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-022-07657-8"  target="_blank">"Zoomed out: digital media use and depersonalization experiences during the COVID-19 lockdown"</a>. <i>Scientific Reports, 12</i>(1), 3888.</p>

<h2>DEPERSONALIZATION SELECTIVELY DISRUPTS BODILY, BUT NOT ABSTRACT, SELF-PRIORITIZATION</h2>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">How does depersonalization change the way the brain processes self-relevant information? To answer this, we used the self-prioritization effect (SPE) — the well-established finding that we are faster to categorize stimuli associated with ourselves compared to stimuli associated with others. Critically, we compared the SPE for two types of self-associated information: <i>bodily</i> (participants' own face) and <i>abstract</i> (arbitrary shapes associated with the self label).</p>

<p style="font-size: 80%;">We found that depersonalization specifically disrupted the self-prioritization of bodily information: individuals higher in depersonalization showed a reduced advantage for their own face, but retained a normal (or even enhanced) advantage for abstract self-associated shapes. This striking double dissociation reveals that the self is not a unitary construct — it is composed of at least two separable components, a bodily self and an abstract conceptual self, which can be differentially affected by dissociative experiences. Depersonalization appears to selectively impair the bodily layer of self-representation while leaving the abstract layer intact.</p>
<p style="font-size: 80%;">Woźniak, M., McEllin, L., Hohwy, J. & Ciaunica, A. (2023). <a href="https://doi.org/10.1037/xhp0001153"  target="_blank">"Depersonalization affects self-prioritization of bodily, but not abstract self-related information"</a>. <i>Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance, 49</i>(11), 1447–1459.</p>
